Consequences of Technological unemployment

4 April 2021 Contentious comments Technology Economics

Scientists like to picture such a utopia, where humans live in eternal happiness, being served by machines, and not having to work. Indeed, many of my friends, who I acquainted from electrical engineering or physics department, seem to take such unlimited optimism as granted, but somehow excluded from the positive air, I always look at the pessimistic side. This is what I don’t understand. Optimists, pray tell me where I get it wrong.

With the advance of robotics, machine learning, artificial intelligence and their like, machines are becoming better and better; they are faster, cheaper, more accurate, and never having to rest. Optimists think humans will excel at more creative jobs; but pessimists like me doubt these jobs are enough in number, or existent at all. To compose a meaningful poem or construct movement in sonata form, computers have a long way to go. But they are more than serviceable to generate background music and automatic replies for a customer service; even doctors and lawyers are seeking computer’s help for finding patterns in cases. To be sure, there are plenty jobs they can qualify, if not all of them at present.

If machines and robots should indeed replace our jobs, optimists gather there will be novel, irreplaceable jobs unimaginable to us by now; yet when pessimists like me ask them what they are, they have no idea. In the Documentary short ‹Humans Need Not Apply›, we imagine two horses are having a conversation as thus. One horse fears that cars are going to replace horses; another horse believes that they no longer has to carry goods and gallop in battlefield, and there will be more effortless jobs awaiting for them. As it turns out, the demand of horse is plain decreasing. Now they are mostly used for sports, movies, and polices. Ridiculous as it is, aren’t human beings dreaming of the same?

If most people are unemployed, to optimists, all we have to do is enjoy the beautiful new world. maybe the surplus resource is so much, that everybody can share, so we no longer have to work, and we may relax, travel, pursue our hobbies at our heart’s desire. Nevertheless, pessimists wonder where our money comes from. Perhaps rephrased more simply, labor, though a torture to some of us, is the only peaceful way to reliably allocate resources. The invisible hand distribute the wealth, not ideally but better than nothing. The fewer jobs there are, the more poor people there are. It is that simple. Machines do produce a lot of things, but these products can’t be sold by then: the bourgeois class is vanishing, and robots don’t purchase.

If the income inequality become heated, Some believe there will be efforts to reorganize the wealth, and maybe there will be unconditional basic income. But what if the society breaks down before that could happen? Democracy took some 150 years, from 1789 to 1945, to mature, and not even peacefully. All the more, we don’t have a hundred years to save the humanity. There will be upheavals both domestic and international. Domestically, social inequality will lead to riots; without traditional jobs, violence is the only remaining way of distribution. We either slay or suffer. Furthermore, as the core countries exploits the marginal countries, fascism will arise during the chaos, internationally, Fascism will arise in periphery countries. And the third world war is a matter of time.

Computers can serve us and save our labour, and it too can undermine our economy and deprive us of our job. But to pessimists like me, it is more an express train, which we think will arrive for the heaven, but falls into hell, and we are too scared to look out of the window. With such risk unparalleled in history, the optimists are like, “the ship will cross the bridge when it comes over”; is that a responsible attitude which scientists should have?
